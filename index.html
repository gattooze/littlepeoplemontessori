“never help a child with a task at which they feel they can succeed.” the hardest thing we do here is nothing.

— Dr. Maria Montessori

A three-year-old buttoning her own coat is doing something enormous. It takes her four minutes. An adult could do it in six seconds — and in doing so, would quietly teach her that she is someone things get done for.

Our mark is three stones balanced on each other. Nothing holds them together but their own weight, found carefully, one on top of the next. That is a childhood. Go on — build ours. Nobody will help you, and that's the point.

The method

six things we believe, and can show you

Montessori is not a philosophy you take on faith. Every one of these is visible in ten minutes on the floor of a classroom — which is exactly why we'd rather you came and looked.

freedom within a form

Children choose their own work, for as long as it holds them. The choice is real; the boundary around it is invisible and absolutely firm.

a room that teaches

Low shelves. One of each material. Real glass, real knives, real weight. The environment does the instructing so the adult doesn't have to.

three years, one room

The five-year-old teaching the three-year-old learns twice. Children stay with the same guide for a full three-year cycle — long enough to be truly known.

the three-hour cycle

An uninterrupted morning. No bells, no rotations, no being pulled off a task that finally clicked. Deep concentration is the rarest thing we protect.

follow the child

We don't run a curriculum on a calendar. We watch what a particular child is reaching for this week, and put the next thing quietly within reach.

the hand teaches the mind

Abstraction comes last, not first. A child understands “a thousand” by carrying the weight of a thousand beads across a room in both arms.

“the greatest sign of success for a teacher is to be able to say: the children are now working as if i did not exist.”

— Dr. Maria Montessori

So we wait. We watch. We keep the room in order and the materials within reach, and we let the four minutes happen. That patience is the whole method, and it is why children who leave us tend to arrive at their next school already believing they are capable.

Moving up

how a child climbs the stack

No entrance tests, no report cards. A child moves up when their guide and the receiving guide agree they're ready — usually in the months around their birthday, always as a visit first.

toddler → primaryAround the third birthday, once independence in dressing, toileting and snack is steady.
primary → the worldAt six — reading, writing, calculating, and carrying the settled belief that they can teach themselves anything.
casa, alongsideDaycare wraps around both environments, so the day has one rhythm instead of three commutes.
A day here

what an uninterrupted morning looks like

The timetable is deliberately boring: one long block, protected fiercely. Everything interesting happens inside it.

3h

One unbroken block. Between 8:45 and 11:45 nothing is scheduled. No circle time called mid-task, no transition bell, no adult deciding it's time to move on.

It looks, to a visitor, like very little is being organised. What's actually happening is the slow arrival of concentration — and once a child finds it, we will not be the ones to break it.

8:30

arrival, at the gate

Each child is greeted by name under the tree, shakes a hand, hangs their own bag and changes their own shoes. Parents say goodbye at the gate — the rooms belong to the children.

8:45

the work cycle opens

Children choose. Some go straight to something hard; some watch from a rug for twenty minutes first. Both are fine. Guides give individual lessons, one child at a time.

10:15

false fatigue, then depth

Mid-morning the room gets noisy and restless. Most schools would call a break here. We hold — and on the other side of it is the deepest, quietest hour of the day.

11:45

coming together

Work is returned to the shelf by the child who took it. Then a short gathering — a song, a story, a problem someone wants the group's help with.

12:15

lunch, laid by the children

Tables set, water poured from glass jugs, spills wiped by whoever spilled. Practical life isn't a subject here; it's just how the day runs.

13:00

under the tree, in any weather

The shaded yard, the planters the children water, the mangoes they count every April and eat every May. Toddlers go home; primary carries on into the afternoon, and casa children stay for the slow, napping hours.

Questions we hear a lot

the honest answers

why don't you publish the fees?

Because a number without context does the conversation a disservice in both directions. Enquire and we'll send you the full fee schedule the same day, along with what it covers — no meeting required, no hard sell.

my child has never been away from us. how do you settle them?

Gradually, and at the child's pace, not a fixed schedule. Short first visits with a parent nearby, stretching as the child's comfort stretches. Most toddlers are settled inside two weeks; some take four; both are normal.

will my child be ready for a conventional school afterwards?

This is the most common worry and the least borne out. Children leave here reading, writing and calculating — but the thing their next teachers actually remark on is that they can start work without being told to.

do you follow a board or curriculum?

We follow the AMI Montessori scope, which comfortably covers — and mostly outruns — state board expectations for these ages. Details of curriculum mapping are shared during the admissions conversation.
